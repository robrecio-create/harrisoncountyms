You're on the Gulf Coast. Seafood is not optional.

## What's Actually Local

The Gulf of Mexico produces shrimp, oysters, crab, and various finfish year-round. "Local" means something specific here: if the shrimp came off a boat that left from Biloxi or Gulfport, it's genuinely local. Ask.

**Gulf brown shrimp** are the backbone of the industry. The season runs roughly May through December. When you see "Gulf shrimp" on a menu, these are usually what they mean.

**Oysters** come from the Mississippi Sound and surrounding waters. They're smaller and saltier than Pacific oysters. Good raw, good grilled with butter and garlic.

**Blue crab** is in season late spring through early fall. Crab claws from Louisiana and Mississippi are sweet and worth every dollar.

**Redfish** (red drum) is the prestige finfish of the Gulf Coast. On the half shell — scaled, but with the skin on, grilled over fire — it's one of the best preparations of any fish anywhere.

## What to Order

- **Boiled shrimp** — simple, messy, delicious; peel them yourself
- **Fried oyster po'boy** — the standard
- **Gumbo** — not seafood-only, but the best versions have Gulf shrimp and crab
- **Crawfish étouffée** — technically Louisiana, but you'll find it everywhere here; it's good
- **Chargrilled oysters** — Drago's in New Orleans made these famous; Gulf Coast versions are solid

## Red Flags

- "Fresh seafood" with no mention of where it came from
- Shrimp that's suspiciously cheap and large (often imported)
- Menus that list "market price" for everything without telling you the market price

## Where to Look

The best local seafood spots tend to be: older, smaller, slightly off the main tourist drag, and full of locals at lunch. The places with the biggest signs and the most prominent Highway 90 positioning are not always the best.

Both [Gulfport](/locations/gulfport/) and [Biloxi](/locations/biloxi/) have strong seafood restaurant concentrations. Pass Christian and Long Beach have quieter options worth finding.
